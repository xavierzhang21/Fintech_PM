Case Study

Training-as-a-Service Product Launch — $112K in Month 1

Designed and commercialized a new corporate TaaS product from vision to market at Avasta Incorporated, generating $112K gross revenue within the first month of launch.

OrganizationAvasta Incorporated · Toronto
RoleManager, Product Design & Management
PeriodJanuary 2021 – March 2022
Impact$112K Gross Revenue · Month 1

Overview

At Avasta Incorporated, I was brought in to lead product design and management across the company's portfolio. The most significant initiative was the design and launch of a brand-new Training-as-a-Service (TaaS) product — a corporate training offering that needed to go from concept to commercial product in a compressed timeline.

The Product

The TaaS product was designed to serve enterprise clients with scalable, modular training programs. I owned the full product lifecycle: from writing the product vision and roadmap, to running usability testing, managing the backlog, and coordinating release management with designers, marketers, sales, and data scientists.

The product generated $112,000 in gross revenue within one month of launch — the fastest commercial validation of any product I had shipped to that point.

Process

I applied a rigorous customer discovery process before a single line of product was built — conducting a Category Study to identify the company's most valuable customer segments and map the highest-leverage UX touchpoints. This upstream work meant the launch was targeted, not speculative.

Go-to-market was coordinated across product, marketing, and sales — with a phased release that prioritized feedback loops from early adopters before scaling outreach.

Key Learnings

  • A strong first month comes from the discovery work done before launch — the product existed in customers' minds before it existed in code
  • Cross-functional clarity is the most underrated launch ingredient — everyone from the designer to the sales rep needs to tell the same story about the product
  • Revenue validation is the only metric that matters in a startup context — everything else is a leading indicator
